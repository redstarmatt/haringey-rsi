The gap between need and provision

Haringey gives every residential road one street sweep per week. But demand for cleaning varies enormously across the borough. Wards in the east — Northumberland Park, Tottenham Hale, Seven Sisters — are far more deprived and record far more police-logged environmental disorder. Yet they receive exactly the same service as Muswell Hill and Highgate.

25× Variation in the RSI score between Haringey’s highest- and lowest-demand wards — all served identically

What needs to change

This is not a request for more money. It is a request for smarter allocation of existing resources. There are two routes to a better outcome — the council could pursue either, or both.

Option 1: Demand-weighted scheduling. Allocate cleaning resource across wards in proportion to demand rather than uniformly. Greenwich moved to this model in 2024, weighting resource towards areas that degrade fastest. Same budget, better outcomes. The RSI provides a starting framework; the council’s own data can refine it.

Option 2: Outcome-based contracting. When the Veolia contract comes up in 2027, specify outcomes — cleanliness to Grade B standard across all wards — rather than inputs. Rather than prescribing how often each street is swept, pay for clean streets and let the contractor decide how to achieve them. The ward-level data this page is built from could form the monitoring baseline.

Either route requires the same foundation:

  1. Publish ward-level cleansing data. The council holds complaint volumes, sweep schedules, and enforcement data by area. Publish it, so residents can see how their ward is served and hold the council to account.
  2. Publish or commission an up-to-date LEQ survey. The last published independent survey was 2017/18 — seven years ago. An independent cleanliness baseline is essential before any contract renegotiation.

The evidence

What the professionals found

In 2017/18, Haringey commissioned Keep Britain Tidy to independently survey street cleanliness across the borough. 640 sites, ten wards, graded on the national NI195 scale. Haringey scored two to three times worse than the London average on every measure: litter, detritus, graffiti, and fly-posting.

The east-west split was unmistakable. Northumberland Park was the worst ward for every element. On Leeside Road, surveyors encountered what they described as one of the most significant fly-tips the surveyor had ever seen. Alexandra Park and Fortis Green fared among the best.

In Noel Park, KBT surveyors recorded “a noticeable difference between Litter standards seen in the Main Retail area and the surrounding streets, caused by considerably different frequencies of cleansing.”

Industry and warehousing areas — concentrated in the east — had 22–45% of sites below acceptable standard. The overall Haringey rating: “Unsatisfactory.”

The fly-tipping crisis

English councils dealt with 1.26 million fly-tipping incidents in 2024/25 — up 9% on the previous year. Street cleansing costs English local authorities an estimated £717–850 million annually. In Haringey, DEFRA recorded over 34,000 fly-tipping incidents in a single year, second only to Enfield. The council’s own 2019 strategy acknowledged the Tottenham High Road corridor as the worst-affected area. In 2024/25, 2,554 fines were issued totalling £1.3 million.

Enforcement alone cannot solve this. Fly-tipping breeds more fly-tipping: when a mattress appears on a corner, it signals that the area is unguarded, and the risk of further dumping rises sharply in the following days. The only effective response is rapid clearance before one incident becomes ten.

Deprivation and litter: what the research says

Haringey is the 6th most deprived borough in London. In the east, more than half of neighbourhoods fall in the 20% most deprived nationally. In the west, almost none. Every major study of street cleanliness finds the same thing: more deprived areas have significantly worse litter and fly-tipping.

Keep Britain Tidy (the national charity that monitors street cleanliness) surveys thousands of sites a year. Their data shows that 28% of sites in the most deprived areas are graded ‘unacceptable,’ compared to just 3% in the least deprived. In Scotland, monitoring found one in five sites had significant litter in the most deprived areas, against one in 44 in the least deprived. A Defra-funded study of fly-tipping across all English councils found deprivation was the single strongest predictor of how much fly-tipping an area gets.

Other boroughs have moved on

In 2024, Greenwich approved demand-weighted cleaning based on how quickly different areas degrade. Higher-demand areas get more resource; lower-demand areas, less. Same budget, smarter allocation. Greenwich estimated savings of £150,000 in year one, rising to £1.2 million by 2028. Philadelphia uses block-level scoring to drive variable cleaning. Haringey still operates the uniform model these authorities have moved away from.

What the council already knows

The council cut sweeping in January 2016, saving £860,000. Performance dropped immediately. They commissioned the KBT survey which told them standards were failing in the east. They may have commissioned further LEQ surveys since — if so, they have not been published. Their fly-tipping strategy acknowledged the geographic concentration. Their public health data acknowledges the overconcentration of fast food outlets in Tottenham. The Veolia contract runs to 2027 — that renegotiation is the moment to shift from input-based to outcome-based KPIs.

Indicators and weights

Two indicators, both derived from published public datasets, selected because they are the two most strongly evidenced predictors of street cleaning demand in the peer-reviewed literature.

Why not more indicators? We tested seven indicators. FixMyStreet report rates were rejected because crowdsourced civic data has a well-documented socio-economic reporting bias: residents in deprived areas suffer from “reporting fatigue” and are statistically less likely to report environmental issues — meaning FixMyStreet systematically undercounts need in the wards that need the most help (confirmed by mySociety’s own analysis of IMD vs. report rates). Population density, food outlet density, station usage, and housing type all correlate with the two retained indicators. Adding them improves precision marginally but at the cost of transparency and robustness.

How the data was processed

IMD deprivation — LSOA-level IMD 2019 overall scores for all 145 Haringey LSOAs. LSOA population-weighted centroids fetched from the ONS Open Geography Portal, then mapped to 2022 wards via point-in-polygon. Ward score is the population-weighted average of constituent LSOA scores. IMD is the official measure of relative deprivation and, per WRAP/Defra analysis, the single strongest predictor of fly-tipping rates across English local authorities. It captures the structural drivers of waste generation: lower vehicle ownership (limiting HWRC access), cost barriers to bulky waste collection, and higher turnover of lower-quality goods in low-income households.

Environmental ASB — Metropolitan Police Service CAD records obtained via FOI (reference M2045). Filtered to ASB Environmental opening type only (excludes ASB Nuisance and ASB Personal). Non-duplicate Haringey records matched to wards using MPS ward codes. Count divided by Census 2021 ward population to give reports per 1,000 residents. 283 environmental ASB incidents across 21 wards. Enviro-ASB functions as a leading indicator of environmental collapse: Routine Activity Theory and Broken Windows Theory both predict (and the data confirms) that visible environmental disorder lowers the threshold for further offending. Parliamentary evidence reviews consistently identify ASB as a precursor to larger-scale fly-tipping. This makes it not just a measure of current conditions but a forecast of near-term deterioration.

Normalisation

Each indicator is normalised to a 0–10 scale using min-max normalisation across Haringey’s 21 wards:

ward_score = ((ward_value − min_value) / (max_value − min_value)) × 10

The ward with the highest raw value on any indicator scores 10, the lowest scores 0, and others are distributed proportionally.

Composite score

RSI = Σ (indicator_normalised_score × indicator_weight)

Result: each ward gets an RSI score from 0 to 10.

Known limitations

IMD vintage. The main Index of Multiple Deprivation used here was published in 2019 using 2015 population estimates. A 2025 update is now being released (the rural report was published March 2026); we will incorporate the full 2025 dataset when it becomes available for all geographies.

LSOA-to-ward mapping. IMD uses 2011 LSOA boundaries; Haringey’s wards were redrawn in 2022. We map LSOAs to wards using ONS population-weighted centroids and point-in-polygon matching, which is a best-fit approximation.

Environmental ASB sample size. Only 283 ASB Environmental records exist for Haringey in the MPS dataset — between 5 and 28 per ward. This small sample means per-capita rates are sensitive to individual incidents. However, the geographic pattern (highest rates in Northumberland Park and Tottenham Central) is consistent with the deprivation gradient.

Reproducibility

All source data, processing scripts, and intermediate files are published alongside this page. The ward boundary GeoJSON, LSOA centroids, IMD scores, and MPS ASB data are all available for download. The processing script calculates every score from raw public data with no manual adjustments. Anyone can audit, challenge, or improve the methodology.

An open invitation. If the council holds better sub-borough data — ward-level complaint volumes, sweep schedules, fly-tipping heat maps, inspection scores — we welcome it. Publish your data, and we will incorporate it. The RSI fills a gap the council has not yet filled. Better data makes a better index.
